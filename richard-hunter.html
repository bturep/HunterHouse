§ 03 The architect

Richard Hunter

1932 — 2023 · Bruce Goff, Norman, Oklahoma · Three built projects

Richard Hunter (1932 — 2023) was a Canadian architect trained under Bruce Goff at the University of Oklahoma. Across a working life of more than six decades he completed three built projects — the Bond House (1965), the Rankin House (1968) and his own Hunter Residence at Prospect Lake (1970 — 2020) — and produced a substantial body of unbuilt work, much of it speculative, all of it retained.

The Hunter Residence is the most extensively documented of the three. It is the only project of which Hunter remained the sole architect, the sole client, and the sole resident across its entire life. The archive of drawings he kept for that house is the principal subject of this Foundation.

01  FormationNorman, Oklahoma · 1955 — 1961

Hunter studied architecture at the University of Oklahoma under Bruce Goff, who chaired the School of Architecture from 1947 to 1956. Goff's pedagogy — a refusal of the International Style orthodoxy, an insistence on the idiosyncratic and the regional, a deep interest in vernacular form — left a lasting impression. The geometric vocabulary visible in Hunter's first independent project, the Bond House, draws directly on the Goff workshop's enthusiasm for the diagonal grid, the centred plan, and the integration of landscape into the building's geometry.

Hunter graduated in 1961 and worked briefly in Goff's Oklahoma practice before moving to British Columbia in 1964.

02  Built workthree projects

1965

Bond House

Oklahoma City

Hunter's first independent commission. Diagonal-grid plan with a centred fireplace mass; a clear inheritance from the Goff workshop.

1968

Rankin House

187 Goward Road, Prospect Lake

Hunter's introduction to the Goward Road context — built immediately north of the parcel that would later become his own home.

1970 — 2020

Hunter Residence

203 Goward Road, Prospect Lake

The fifty-year project. Two architecturally distinct wings, built a generation apart, with a tower, skywalk, and garden interventions extending into Hunter's late eighties.

04  Exhibitions & publications

Hunter's work was rarely exhibited during his lifetime. Two notable inclusions: the 1977 group show Architects of British Columbia at the Vancouver Art Gallery (Rankin House drawings); and the 2003 retrospective Bruce Goff & the American School at the National Building Museum, Washington DC (Bond House plans).

A monograph, Hunter House: Fifty Years, is in preparation by the Foundation.
