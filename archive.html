About the Archive

A structured catalogue of a single house, drawn over fifty years.

The Hunter House Archive is a structured catalogue of drawings, photographs, and related materials documenting the design and continuous revision of the Hunter Residence. It draws from six source collections, spans materials from 1968 to 2020, and is designed to grow as additional collections are processed.

The collections

The archive recognizes six source collections, each representing a distinct provenance. The complementary character of the two largest is significant: Hunter donated his original 1970–74 build documentation to the Canadian Architectural Archives and kept the working files for everything that came after. Together they form a continuous fifty-year drawing record of a single property.

Hunter House Collection

Custodian: Hunter House Foundation

Richard Hunter's own working files — 110 architectural drawings made for the house from 1979 onward, together with surveys, engineering drawings commissioned from external firms, and ephemeral materials. The primary collection. Held by the family, transferred to the Foundation with the sale of the house in 2023, and scanned for this archive.

Canadian Architectural Archives

Custodian: University of Calgary · Accession 2019.61

The Richard Hunter fonds at the CAA: 344 architectural drawings, 62 photographs, 0.22 linear meters of textual records, predominantly 1955–2010. The Hunter Residence materials include the original 1970–74 build drawings, seven colour studies for the 1990s addition, and a run of family photographs documenting the house in its early years. John Fulker Collection

Custodian: West Vancouver Museum

Photographs of the Hunter Residence taken by Victoria photographer John Fulker; the professional documentation of the original structure.

Eric Gessinger Collection

How items are identified

Every item in the archive receives a permanent, unique Archive ID at the time of ingestion. Archive IDs are permanent — once assigned, an ID is never reused, reassigned, or altered. The number encodes intake order, not chronological or topical sequence: a photograph catalogued in 2026 may hold a higher number than a drawing made in 1969.

How the catalogue works

The archive is maintained as a structured database with a controlled vocabulary. Drawings are classified by type (plan, elevation, section, detail, framing plan, and so on), by use (construction, design development, construction for permit, survey, speculative), by built status (built, unbuilt, partially built, built and removed), and by the area of the house they address. Each item is grouped into a project set — the design or construction phase to which it belongs.

The structured catalogue allows the archive to be read at multiple resolutions. A drawing can be located by date, by area, by phase, by drawing type, or by the project set it belongs to. The same drawing can participate in multiple groupings without redundancy.

Access

The catalogue is open and queryable. The data is published as a Wikibase, supporting both browsing through this site and direct SPARQL queries against the underlying graph.

  • Browse the collection — every item in the Wikibase, grouped by type
  • hunterhouse.wikibase.cloud — the structured data layer
  • SPARQL endpoint — for graph queries

Rights, reproduction conditions, and provenance information are recorded per item. Items marked private are not visible in this catalogue; only items cleared for public display appear here.
